Section 1 · Foundations

The Evolution of Language Teaching Methods

📜
From Classical Languages to Modern Communication
Language teaching methods have evolved dramatically over the past 200 years. Understanding HOW and WHY methods changed helps you choose the right approach for your classroom.

This lesson covers the three foundational methods that dominated language teaching from the 1800s to 1970s. Each method reflects a different theory of language and learning.

1800s – 1950s
Grammar-Translation Method (GTM)
Focus on reading, writing, translation, and grammar rules. Heavy use of L1.
1880s – 1930s
Direct Method (DM)
Reaction to GTM. No L1, oral practice, meaning through demonstration.
1940s – 1970s
Audio-Lingual Method (ALM)
Based on Behaviourism. Drills, habit formation, oral fluency.

What You'll Master

Grammar-Translation — Reading, writing, translation, explicit grammar
Direct Method — Target language only, oral practice, no translation
Audio-Lingual — Drills, pattern practice, habit formation
Key Comparisons — L1 use, skills focus, teacher role, theory base
Section 2 · Grammar-Translation Method

Grammar-Translation Method — The Classical Approach

📖
Reading Literature, Mastering Grammar
Originally designed for teaching Latin and Greek, GTM focuses on reading literary texts, learning grammar rules explicitly, and translating between L1 and L2.

Core Principles of GTM

Goal: Read literary texts and understand grammar
Skills focus: Reading and writing (NOT speaking/listening)
L1 use: Heavy — instruction, translation, explanations in native language
Grammar: Explicit, deductive (rules first, then examples)
Vocabulary: Bilingual word lists, memorization

Typical GTM Lesson: Teacher explains a grammar rule in L1 → Students memorize the rule → Students translate sentences from L2 to L1 or vice versa → Teacher corrects errors explicitly.

GTM Techniques

1. Translation — L1 ↔ L2 passages
2. Grammar analysis — Parse sentences, identify parts of speech
3. Memorization — Vocabulary lists, verb conjugations
4. Reading comprehension — Literary texts, questions in L1
5. Fill-in-the-blank — Grammar exercises
✅ Strengths

Builds strong reading skills • Good for grammar analysis • Works with large classes • Little teacher training needed • Good for academic/literary goals

❌ Limitations

Neglects speaking/listening • No communicative competence • Boring for many students • No real-world language use • Creates passive learners

⚠️Exam Trap: GTM is NOT based on Behaviourism. It predates behaviourist psychology. GTM is based on classical/traditional approaches to studying Latin and Greek.
Section 3 · Direct Method

Direct Method — Natural Language Learning

🗣️
Target Language Only — Like a Child Learns
The Direct Method was a REACTION against GTM. It tried to mimic how children learn their first language — through direct association between words and meaning, without translation.

Core Principles of Direct Method

Goal: Oral fluency and natural communication
Skills focus: Speaking and listening (oral skills first)
L1 use: PROHIBITED — target language only
Grammar: Implicit, inductive (examples first, then discover rules)
Meaning: Through demonstration, objects, pictures — NOT translation

Key Figure: Maximilian Berlitz commercialized the Direct Method through his language schools. Also associated with the Reform Movement of the 1880s (Sweet, Viëtor, Jespersen).

Direct Method Techniques

1. Demonstration — Use objects, pictures, actions to convey meaning
2. Question-Answer — Full sentences in target language
3. Reading aloud — Pronunciation practice
4. Conversation practice — Natural dialogues
5. Self-correction — Students discover their own errors
✅ Strengths

Develops oral skills • Natural language use • Pronunciation focus • Mimics L1 acquisition • Motivating for some learners

❌ Limitations

Requires fluent teachers • Difficult with abstract vocabulary • Time-consuming • Hard in large classes • Neglects reading/writing

💡Memory Tip: Direct Method = "DIRECT" connection between word and meaning (no L1 translation). Teacher demonstrates, shows, acts out — never translates.
Section 4 · Audio-Lingual Method

Audio-Lingual Method — Drills & Habits

🔊
Language is a Habit — Drill Until Automatic
ALM was developed during WWII to train soldiers quickly. Based on BEHAVIOURISM, it treats language as a set of habits formed through stimulus-response drilling.

Core Principles of ALM

Theory base: Structural Linguistics + Behaviourism
Goal: Automatic oral responses (habit formation)
Skills sequence: Listen → Speak → Read → Write
L1 use: Minimal — avoided to prevent interference
Grammar: Implicit, through pattern drills (not rules)
Errors: Prevented at all costs (errors = bad habits)

Key Figures: Charles Fries (structural linguistics) and Nelson Brooks (method design). ALM dominated US language teaching in the 1950s-60s.

ALM Drill Types (Memorize These!)

Repetition Drill — Teacher: "I go to school." Students: "I go to school."
Substitution Drill — "I go to school." → "I go to work." (substitute one element)
Transformation Drill — "She reads." → "She is reading." (change form)
Chain Drill — Student A asks B → B answers then asks C → etc.
Backward Build-up — "...school" → "...to school" → "...go to school" → "I go to school"

ALM Techniques

1. Dialogue memorization — Model dialogue, repeat, memorize
2. Pattern drills — Repetition, substitution, transformation
3. Minimal pair practice — "ship" vs "sheep" (pronunciation)
4. Language lab — Audio equipment for individual practice
5. Immediate reinforcement — Praise for correct responses

ALM Limitations

Mechanical — No creativity, boring for students
No meaning focus — Students can drill without understanding
Transfer failure — Lab skills don't transfer to real communication
Theory problems — Chomsky's critique of Behaviourism (1959)
Student passivity — Teacher-centered, limited student initiative
⚠️Exam Trap: ALM is based on BEHAVIOURISM (Skinner), NOT Cognitivism or Innatism. Chomsky's attack on Behaviourism contributed to ALM's decline.
Section 5 · Method Comparison

Comparing the Three Methods

Use this comparison table for quick exam review. Know the differences in L1 use, skills focus, and theory base.

FeatureGTMDirect MethodALM
Theory BaseClassical traditionNatural acquisitionBehaviourism + Structuralism
L1 UseHeavyProhibitedMinimal
Skills FocusReading, writingSpeaking, listeningListening, speaking (oral first)
GrammarExplicit, deductiveImplicit, inductiveImplicit, through drills
Error ApproachExplicit correctionSelf-correctionPrevention (errors = bad habits)
Teacher RoleAuthority, explainerDemonstrator, modelConductor, model
Main TechniqueTranslationDemonstrationPattern drills
VocabularyBilingual listsContext, picturesLimited, in patterns
💡Quick Memory:
• GTM = Translation, L1, reading/writing, explicit grammar
• DM = No L1, demonstration, oral, inductive
• ALM = Drills, habit formation, behaviourism, oral

Key Contrasts to Remember

GTM → L1 is the medium of instruction
DM → L1 is forbidden
ALM → L1 is avoided (causes interference)
Section 6 · Classroom Application

Recognizing Methods in the Classroom

In the exam, you might need to identify which method a teacher is using based on a classroom scenario. Practice with these examples:

🎯
Match the Scenario to the Method
Scenario A: Teacher explains the past tense rule in Arabic, then students translate sentences from English to Arabic. → Method?

Scenario B: Teacher holds up an apple and says "apple" repeatedly. Students repeat. No Arabic is used. → Method?

Scenario C: Teacher plays an audio dialogue. Students repeat in chorus. Then they do substitution drills changing one word each time. → Method?

Scenario D: Students memorize a dialogue and then perform transformation drills: "I eat" → "Do I eat?" → Method?

Scenario E: Teacher uses gestures and pictures to explain "happy" without ever saying the Arabic word. → Method?
✅Answers:
A = GTM (L1 explanation + translation)
B = Direct Method (target language only, demonstration)
C = ALM (audio, drills, repetition)
D = ALM (dialogue memorization + transformation drills)
E = Direct Method (no L1, pictures/gestures)

Modern Relevance

GTM — Still used for academic/literary language courses
DM — Influenced immersion programs and CLT
ALM — Some techniques (drills) still useful for pronunciation and automaticity

🎙️ Listen — A Trainer Explains the Three Methods

Press ▶ to listen to a teacher trainer explaining the three methods. Identify which method she is describing in each part.

A Trainer Compares GTM, DM, and ALM
~55 seconds · British English

Trainer: "Let me explain the three classical methods. The first method focuses on reading literary texts and translating them. Grammar rules are explained in the students' native language, and they memorize vocabulary through bilingual word lists. Speaking and listening are not priorities."

"The second method was a reaction against translation-based teaching. Teachers use ONLY the target language. They demonstrate meaning through objects, pictures, and actions. Students learn to speak by speaking — there's no translation at all."

"The third method was developed for military training during World War II. It's based on the idea that language is a habit. Students listen to model dialogues, repeat them in chorus, and practice through pattern drills like substitution and transformation. The goal is automatic, rapid oral responses."

"A key difference: the first method uses the native language heavily, the second bans it completely, and the third avoids it because it's believed to cause interference with the new habits being formed."

Section 8 · Interview Preparation

Interview Practice — Compare the Methods

ℹ️Note: The written exam is 100% multiple choice (A, B, C, D, E). However, in the oral interview, you may be asked to compare methods. This section prepares you for that.

Practice explaining the key differences between GTM and ALM in 60–80 words. Focus on skills, L1 use, and theory base.

✍️Structure your answer:
1. Name both methods
2. Skills focus (GTM = reading/writing; ALM = speaking/listening)
3. L1 use (GTM = heavy; ALM = avoided)
4. Theory base (GTM = classical; ALM = Behaviourism)
5. Main technique (GTM = translation; ALM = drills)
0 words (aim for 60–80)
✅ Good practice!

Model Answer:
"Grammar-Translation Method (GTM) and Audio-Lingual Method (ALM) differ in several key ways. GTM focuses on reading and writing skills, using translation and explicit grammar rules, while ALM prioritizes listening and speaking through pattern drills. Regarding L1 use, GTM uses the native language heavily as the medium of instruction, whereas ALM avoids L1 to prevent interference with new habits. GTM comes from classical language teaching traditions, while ALM is based on Behaviourist psychology. GTM's main technique is translation; ALM's is repetition drilling."
