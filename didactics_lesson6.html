Section 1 · Foundations

The Communicative Revolution

🌍
From Form to Function — Language for Real Communication
In the 1970s-80s, language teaching shifted from focusing on grammatical forms to focusing on COMMUNICATION. The question changed from "Is it grammatically correct?" to "Can students use language to communicate meaningfully?"

This lesson covers three interconnected approaches that dominate modern language teaching: Communicative Language Teaching (CLT), Task-Based Learning (TBL), and the Natural Approach.

Section 2 · Communicative Language Teaching

CLT — Language for Real Communication

💬
Knowing a Language Means Using It
CLT emerged in the 1970s as a reaction against grammar-focused methods. It shifted the goal from linguistic accuracy to COMMUNICATIVE COMPETENCE — the ability to use language appropriately in real situations.

Core Principles of CLT

Goal: Develop communicative competence (not just linguistic accuracy)
Focus: Meaning and function over form
Materials: Authentic texts and real-world tasks
Interaction: Pair work, group work, information gaps
Teacher role: Facilitator, not controller
Errors: Tolerated if communication is successful

Key Figure: Dell Hymes (1972) introduced "communicative competence" — knowing WHAT to say, to WHOM, WHEN, and HOW. Later expanded by Canale & Swain (1980) into four components.

CLT Key Features

1. Information gap activities — Students have different information and must communicate to complete tasks
2. Choice — Students choose what to say and how
3. Feedback — Communication success is the measure (Did you understand?)
4. Authentic materials — Real texts, not simplified textbook language
5. Fluency AND accuracy — Both matter, but fluency often prioritized
⚠️Exam Trap: CLT does NOT ignore grammar. It teaches grammar in CONTEXT, for communicative purposes — not as isolated rules. CLT balances fluency AND accuracy.
Section 3 · Communicative Competence

The Four Components of Communicative Competence

Canale & Swain (1980) expanded Hymes' concept into four components. Tap each card to reveal details. These are heavily tested!

📝
Linguistic Competence
Tap to reveal...

Grammar, vocabulary, pronunciation. Knowing the rules of the language system — what words to use, how to form sentences, correct pronunciation.

🎭
Sociolinguistic Competence
Tap to reveal...

Appropriate use in context. Knowing what's appropriate for different situations — formal vs informal, polite vs casual, different social roles.

🔗
Discourse Competence
Tap to reveal...

Cohesion and coherence. Connecting sentences into meaningful texts — using linking words, organizing ideas logically, maintaining topic flow.

🔧
Strategic Competence
Tap to reveal...

Repair and compensation strategies. Fixing communication breakdowns — paraphrasing, asking for clarification, using gestures when words fail.

Memory Framework

Linguistic = WHAT words/grammar (form)
Sociolinguistic = WHEN/WHERE to use them (context)
Discourse = HOW to connect them (text)
Strategic = WHAT TO DO when it breaks down (repair)
💡Exam Tip: Linguistic competence alone is NOT enough for real communication. A grammatically perfect sentence can be socially inappropriate or disconnected from context.
Section 4 · CLT Techniques

CLT Classroom Techniques

Information Gap Activities

Definition: One student has information another needs; they must communicate to share it
Purpose: Creates genuine need for communication
Examples: Partner A has a map with some places; Partner B has other places → describe to complete both maps

Information gaps create authentic communication because there's a real reason to talk — you don't know what your partner knows.

Role Play & Simulation

Role Play: Students take on roles (customer, waiter) and interact in scenarios
Simulation: More complex, extended role plays mimicking real situations
Benefits: Practice sociolinguistic competence, real-world language functions

Example: "You are a tourist asking for directions. Your partner is a local who gives directions using a map."

Authentic Materials

Definition: Real texts not created for teaching (newspapers, menus, tickets, websites)
Purpose: Expose students to real language as used by native speakers
Challenge: May be difficult for beginners; needs scaffolding

Authentic materials prepare students for real-world language — not the simplified, artificial language of textbooks.

Section 5 · Task-Based Learning

Task-Based Learning — Learning by Doing

🎯
The Task is Central — Language is the Tool
In TBL, students learn language BY completing meaningful tasks. The focus is on the OUTCOME of the task, not on practicing specific language forms. Language emerges naturally from task demands.

Key Figures: Jane Willis (Willis Framework) and N.S. Prabhu (Bangalore Project). TBL is considered a "strong" version of CLT.

What is a "Task"?

Definition: An activity where language is used to achieve a real-world outcome
Focus: Meaning and communication, NOT form
Examples: Plan a trip, solve a problem, make a decision, create something
NOT a task: Grammar exercises, drills, controlled practice

Willis's TBL Framework — Three Stages (Tap to expand)

1
Pre-Task
Tap to expand...

Introduction: Teacher introduces topic and task. Activates background knowledge. May provide useful vocabulary. Students understand what they need to do.

2
Task Cycle
Tap to expand...

Task: Students do the task in pairs/groups.
Planning: Prepare to report to class.
Report: Share outcomes with class. Focus on MEANING.

3
Language Focus
Tap to expand...

Analysis: Examine language used in task.
Practice: Focus on specific forms that emerged. Grammar comes AFTER communication, not before.

⚠️Exam Trap — TBL vs PPP:
• PPP (Presentation-Practice-Production) = Form FIRST, then use
• TBL = Task FIRST, then analyze form
TBL is task-first; PPP is grammar-first.
Section 6 · Natural Approach

Natural Approach — Krashen & Terrell

🌱
Acquisition Over Learning — Comprehensible Input
The Natural Approach (Krashen & Terrell, 1983) applies Krashen's Monitor Model to classroom teaching. Focus on ACQUISITION through comprehensible input in a low-anxiety environment.

Core Principles

Input Hypothesis: Provide comprehensible input at i+1 level
Affective Filter: Create low-anxiety, supportive environment
Silent Period: Allow learners time to listen before speaking
Grammar: Acquired naturally, not explicitly taught
Errors: Not corrected directly (acquisition is unconscious)

Key Figures: Stephen Krashen (theory) and Tracy Terrell (classroom application). The Natural Approach is the practical application of Krashen's five hypotheses.

Natural Approach Techniques

TPR-like activities — Physical responses to commands
Picture stories — Input through visual narratives
Affective activities — Personal topics, low-stress discussion
Extended listening — Stories, descriptions before production
No forced output — Students speak when ready

Strengths

✅ Low anxiety promotes risk-taking
✅ Respects natural acquisition order
✅ Student-centered, meaningful communication
✅ Builds listening comprehension before speaking

Limitations

❌ Difficult to measure i+1 precisely
❌ May neglect explicit grammar for some learners
❌ Silent period may frustrate some students/teachers
❌ Less effective for academic/professional language needs
💡Connection: Natural Approach techniques appear in TPR (Total Physical Response) and content-based instruction. The key link is comprehensible input and low anxiety.

A Trainer Compares CLT, TBL & Natural Approach
~55 seconds · British English

Trainer: "Let me explain three approaches that transformed language teaching. The first approach focuses on developing communicative competence — not just grammar knowledge, but the ability to use language appropriately in real situations. Students work in pairs and groups, often with information gap activities where they must communicate to complete tasks."

"The second approach takes this further by making the TASK central to everything. Students complete a meaningful task first — like planning a trip or solving a problem — and then analyze the language that emerged. Grammar comes AFTER the task, not before."

"The third approach is based on the idea that we acquire language best through comprehensible input — language slightly above our current level. The classroom should have low anxiety, and students aren't forced to speak until they're ready. There's a silent period where learners just listen and absorb."

"All three share important features: they're learner-centered, they prioritize meaning over form, and they see communication as both the means and the goal of learning."
