Section 1 · Foundations

What is Learning?

🧠
The Most Important Question in Education
Before we can talk about how to teach, we must understand what learning actually IS. Every teaching method is built on a theory of how learning happens — and your exam will test whether you understand those foundations.

The definition of learning that appears most frequently in teaching exams comes from Wakefield (1996):

The Definition of Learning

"A relatively permanent change in behaviour based on experience or discovery."
— Wakefield, 1996

This definition has three critical elements that examiners look for:

ElementWhat it meansWhy it matters
Relatively permanentNot just a temporary reactionLearning lasts — it changes future behaviour
Change in behaviourSomething observable happensBehaviourists focus on this — they only study what can be seen
Based on experienceThe environment shapes learningPractice, repetition, and reinforcement drive the change
🔎Why study learning theories? Every teaching method has a psychological foundation. If you understand why Behaviourism believes learning = habit formation, you will instantly understand the Audio-Lingual Method, TPR, and error correction strategies — because they all come from the same root.
💡The 3 main theories you must know: (1) Behaviourism — habit formation through stimulus-response. (2) Cognitivism / Innatism — mental processing and rule discovery. (3) Constructivism — building knowledge through experience. This lesson covers Behaviourism in full.

Section 3 · Pavlov & Watson

Classical Conditioning — Learning by Association

Classical Conditioning is the foundation of how we associate stimuli with responses. It explains why students can feel anxious about grammar tests, or why a particular classroom routine makes them feel focused and ready.

💡The key insight: a neutral stimulus (the bell) becomes meaningful through repeated pairing with a natural stimulus (food). After enough repetitions, the neutral stimulus alone produces the response.

🧠Watson added that emotions are also classically conditioned. Fear, enjoyment, and anxiety about subjects are all learned associations — not fixed personality traits. This is why a bad experience in one English class can make a student afraid of all English classes.

Classical Conditioning gives teachers three powerful tools:

Classroom Applications

1. Pairing praise with tasks: Consistently praising students during grammar activities creates a positive association → grammar becomes enjoyable, not frightening.
2. Using music or routines: Playing the same music at the start of every lesson conditions students to feel focused and ready. The music becomes the "bell."
3. Reducing anxiety: If students feel anxious about speaking, pair speaking tasks with low-pressure, fun activities (games, pair work) to build positive associations.
🎯Exam tip: When asked "how can you apply Classical Conditioning in the classroom?", always give a specific example that shows the pairing: neutral stimulus + natural response = new conditioned response.

Section 4 · B.F. Skinner

Operant Conditioning — Behaviour Shaped by Consequences

While Pavlov and Watson focused on what triggers behaviour, Skinner focused on what happens after the behaviour. He argued that the consequences of an action determine whether it will be repeated — and this is what makes language learning a form of habit formation.

Skinner's Core Formula

Behaviour → Consequence → Change in future behaviour
"Reinforced actions are more likely to be repeated." — B.F. Skinner

Skinner identified four types of consequences. Tap each card to understand it fully:

➕ Positive Reinforcement
Adding something pleasant after a behaviour → increases the behaviour.
"She answered correctly → Teacher says 'Excellent!' → She answers more often."
Most used in CLT
➖ Negative Reinforcement
Removing something unpleasant after a behaviour → increases the behaviour.
"Student stops being anxious when the teacher removes timed pressure → attends more."
Often confused with punishment
⛔ Positive Punishment
Adding something unpleasant after a behaviour → decreases the behaviour.
"Student speaks L1 → Teacher gives extra homework → Student uses L2 more."
Used in ALM (error correction)
🚫 Extinction
Removing reinforcement → behaviour eventually disappears.
"Teacher stops reacting to attention-seeking behaviour → behaviour fades."
Classroom management tool
⚠️The most confused pair in exams: Negative Reinforcement ≠ Punishment. Negative reinforcement REMOVES something bad to INCREASE behaviour. Punishment ADDS something bad to DECREASE behaviour. Both use the word "negative/punishment" — but they do opposite things.

Skinner's Box Experiment — The Rat

Rat presses lever → receives food → positive reinforcement → presses lever more often
Rat presses lever → electric shock stops → negative reinforcement → presses lever more often
Rat presses lever → receives shock → positive punishment → presses lever less often
🧠Watson vs Skinner — key exam distinction:
Watson focused on the stimulus that produces a response — learning is passive.
Skinner focused on the behaviour itself and its consequences — learning is active.
Watson: "Something happens to the learner." Skinner: "What the learner does determines what they learn next."

Section 5 · Edward Thorndike

Thorndike — Trial, Error & The Law of Effect

Thorndike's work was the bridge between early stimulus-response psychology and modern learning theory. His famous experiment with a cat in a puzzle box revealed how learning progresses through repeated attempts.

Escapes faster each time
Habit Formed
💡Key insight: The cat didn't understand — it simply repeated what worked. This is learning without insight — purely through consequence. Successful actions get stronger; useless ones disappear.

Law of Effect

Responses followed by satisfying outcomes are strengthened and more likely to be repeated.
Responses followed by unsatisfying outcomes are weakened and less likely to recur.

Law of Use

The more a connection is practised and used, the stronger it becomes.
Example: Regular grammar drills strengthen the habit of correct use.

Law of Disuse

Connections that are not practised become weaker over time.
Example: A student who stops practising vocabulary will forget it — the connection fades.

Thorndike's laws translate directly into language teaching strategies:

LawTeaching ApplicationExample
Law of EffectReward correct language usePraise, stars, positive feedback after correct answers
Law of UseRepeat and practise regularlyDaily vocabulary revision, weekly grammar recycling
Law of DisuseReview forgotten contentSpaced repetition, revision exercises before exams
🧠Exam insight: Thorndike's Law of Effect is essentially the same idea as Skinner's reinforcement — but Thorndike came FIRST (1898 vs 1930s). Examiners sometimes ask you to compare them: Thorndike focused on the "satisfying result" itself; Skinner was more systematic about types of reinforcement.
Section 6 · Applied Behaviourism

Behaviourism in the Language Classroom

Behaviourism didn't stay in the laboratory — it directly shaped language teaching methods. Understanding this connection is one of the most valuable skills for your teaching exam.

🔗The direct link: Behaviourism → Audio-Lingual Method (ALM). The ALM was built entirely on the idea that language learning = habit formation through stimulus-response-reinforcement. This is why ALM uses drills, repetition, immediate correction, and praise.

Scenario C: A student practices vocabulary flashcards every day for a week, stops for a month, and then forgets most of the words.

B → Operant Conditioning / Positive Punishment + Error as bad habit (Skinner / ALM principle)
C → Law of Disuse (Thorndike) — unused connections weaken
D → Positive Reinforcement (Skinner) — praise increases the behaviour
⚖️
Critical Thinking — Limitations of Behaviourism
Consider this question carefully — it often appears in the written exam:
"Behaviourism has been both praised and criticised in language teaching. Discuss two strengths and two limitations of applying Behaviourist principles in the EFL classroom."
🧠Model answer points:
Strengths: (1) Provides clear, structured practice through drills — good for pronunciation and pattern learning. (2) Positive reinforcement builds student confidence and motivation.
Limitations: (1) Language is creative — humans produce sentences they have never heard (Chomsky's critique). Behaviourism cannot explain this. (2) Mechanical drills ("drill and kill") are boring and don't develop communicative competence for real-world use.
